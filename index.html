Google and Amazon's AI 'Profits' Actually Come From Their Anthropic Stake

What happened: Nearly half of Alphabet's record $62.6 billion profit last quarter came from marking up its Anthropic equity stake, not from actual business operations. Amazon similarly disclosed that $16.8 billion—more than half its pre-tax income—came from unrealized gains on its Anthropic investment. When the companies invest more in Anthropic or commit cloud spending, it pushes Anthropic's valuation higher, which lets them book the increase as profit without Anthropic paying them a dollar.

Why it matters: Big Tech spent $130.65 billion on AI capital expenditures in Q1 2026 alone—more than three times the inflation-adjusted cost of the Manhattan Project—and plan to spend nearly $700 billion this year. The accounting raises questions about whether the massive AI buildout is generating real returns or just paper gains from circular investments between cloud providers and the AI labs that depend on them.

What's next: Anthropic's reported talks at a $900 billion valuation suggest the next markup could be even larger. As both companies race toward potential IPOs later this year, investors will scrutinize whether AI revenue can justify valuations built partly on self-reinforcing investment cycles.

What's Contentious

Meta Deploys AI to Scan Photos for Bone Structure to Catch Underage Users

What happened: Meta announced it will use AI to scan photos and videos for visual clues like height and bone structure to detect users under 13 on Facebook and Instagram, with the system now operating in select countries. The company also uses AI to analyze profiles for contextual clues like birthday celebrations or school grades across posts, comments, bios, and captions, with plans to expand the technology to Instagram Live and Facebook Groups.

Why it matters: The announcement comes weeks after a New Mexico jury ordered Meta to pay $375 million in civil penalties for misleading consumers about platform safety and putting children at risk. The use of biometric AI analysis on user-generated content to infer age raises new privacy questions, even as Meta frames it as a child safety measure. If the system flags someone as potentially underage, their account gets deactivated unless they can prove their age.

What's next: Users flagged by the system will need to go through Meta's age verification process to prevent permanent account deletion. The broader rollout timeline and whether other platforms adopt similar biometric scanning remains to be seen, as does regulatory response to companies using AI to analyze physical characteristics of users without explicit consent.

Apple Plans iOS 27 'Extensions' to Let Users Pick Third-Party AI Models

What happened: Bloomberg reports that when iOS 27 is released later this year, iPhone users will be able to choose from multiple third-party large language models to power various functions, with the new 'Extensions' feature allowing users to access generative AI capabilities from installed apps on demand through Apple Intelligence features like Siri, Writing Tools, and Image Playground. The capability will also be available for iPadOS 27 and macOS 27, with models from Google and Anthropic currently being tested.

Why it matters: With longtime CEO Tim Cook soon stepping down, incoming executive John Ternus is tasked with charting Apple's AI strategy, and while the company is perceived as 'behind' on AI compared to peers, its plan focuses less on building AI infrastructure and more on turning existing hardware into an AI-centric experience for users. This modular approach positions Apple as a platform for AI models rather than competing directly with model makers.

What's next: The move could reshape the mobile AI landscape by turning iPhones into neutral ground where OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, and others compete for user preference. It also sets up a contrast with rivals who are building vertically integrated AI stacks, potentially making Apple the Switzerland of the AI wars.

AI's Idealistic Origins Face Reality as Musk Trial and Pentagon Deals Unfold

What happened: In testimony Monday at the Musk v. OpenAI trial, OpenAI president Greg Brockman acknowledged launching OpenAI as a nonprofit to 'benefit humanity as a whole' while also acknowledging his stake in OpenAI's for-profit arm may now be worth $20 billion to $30 billion. OpenAI and Anthropic were founded on the idea that AI would prioritize safety and the public good, but those principles are giving way to an arms race for market share as companies release ever more powerful models.

Why it matters: AI behemoths are now locked in escalating competition for enterprise, consumer and government business, and when the Pentagon blacklisted Anthropic for wanting to restrict how its AI could be used—including for mass surveillance and autonomous weapons—the company reversed course. The widening gap between founding missions and commercial reality is playing out in real time as both companies race toward IPOs potentially worth hundreds of billions.

What's next: Testimony in the Musk trial continues this week to determine if OpenAI's structural change compromised or preserved its original mission. The outcome could set precedent for how courts view the transformation of mission-driven AI labs into commercial giants, while investors weigh whether safety commitments are compatible with the capital demands of the AI race.

Policy Alert

Trump Administration Weighs AI Safety Testing After Anthropic's Mythos

What happened: The Trump administration is considering a plan requiring the Pentagon to safety test AI models deployed to federal, state and local governments, marking a reevaluation of its hard line against AI safety measures in a post-Mythos world. The White House Office of the National Cyber Director hosted meetings last week with tech companies and trade groups to discuss security concerns raised by advanced models including Anthropic's Mythos Preview, and has been discussing an AI security framework for Pentagon-led testing.

Why it matters: While Trump immediately revoked the Biden AI executive order on Day 1, many ideas now being considered already existed in that order, including having the Commerce Department's Center for AI Standards and Innovation review internal security tests before model releases. The Commerce Department announced Tuesday it will conduct pre-deployment testing of AI models from Google, Microsoft and xAI, marking a pivot in the White House's earlier approach to AI policy.

What's next: Sources say talks at the White House remain fluid with specific proposals changing often, with real tensions being worked through internally between economic and tech policy voices worried about complicating deployments and the national security community concerned about AI-enabled cyberattacks. Whether this leads to binding regulation or voluntary frameworks remains unclear.

Samsung Hits $1 Trillion Valuation on Booming AI Chip Demand

What happened: Samsung Electronics Co. has reached a $1 trillion market valuation following booming demand for its chips used in artificial intelligence. The South Korean tech giant joins an exclusive club of companies valued above this threshold, driven primarily by its semiconductor business supplying AI infrastructure.

Why it matters: Samsung's milestone underscores how the AI boom is creating winners beyond the usual suspects of Nvidia and hyperscalers. As a major supplier of memory chips and other components essential to AI data centers, Samsung is capitalizing on the massive capital expenditure wave without needing to build AI models or applications itself. The valuation also reflects investor confidence that AI infrastructure spending will continue despite questions about return on investment.

What's next: Samsung's path forward depends on maintaining its manufacturing edge as competitors like SK Hynix and Micron also vie for AI chip market share. The company must also navigate geopolitical tensions around semiconductor supply chains while meeting surging demand from data center operators racing to build out AI capacity.
