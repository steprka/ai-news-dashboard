Microsoft's AI Discovers New Antibiotics That Kill Superbugs

The breakthrough: Microsoft Research announced that its BioGPT-7 model has successfully identified three new antibiotic compounds that show remarkable effectiveness against drug-resistant bacteria, including MRSA and carbapenem-resistant Enterobacteriaceae. The AI screened over 100 million molecular structures in just 48 hours, a process that would typically take human researchers decades to complete.

The results: Early lab tests show the AI-discovered compounds are 10x more potent than existing antibiotics against superbugs, with minimal side effects on healthy human cells. What's next: Pharmaceutical giants Pfizer and Roche are reportedly in bidding wars to license the compounds, while Microsoft plans to open-source the underlying AI model to accelerate global drug discovery. This marks the first time an AI system has independently discovered a new class of life-saving drugs from scratch.

What's Contentious

Parents Sue School District Over AI Tutor Recording Kids' Voices

The lawsuit: A class-action lawsuit filed in Texas alleges that the Houston ISD's AI tutoring system, used by 200,000 students, has been secretly recording and storing children's voices without parental consent. The AI system, developed by EduTech startup Scholars AI, was supposed to only analyze text responses but parents discovered voice recordings in data breach documents.

The violations: Legal experts say the recordings violate both COPPA (Children's Online Privacy Protection Act) and state biometric privacy laws, potentially exposing the district to $50 million in fines. Parents are particularly outraged that the AI vendor has been using the voice data to train commercial speech recognition models sold to other companies.

The fallout: Houston ISD has suspended the AI tutoring program affecting 450 schools, while similar systems in Chicago and Miami are under investigation. Privacy advocates are calling for federal oversight of AI systems in schools, arguing that children's data is being treated as a 'free resource' for AI training.

Policy Alert

Singapore Launches Mandatory AI Impact Assessments for All Models

The regulation: Singapore's new AI Governance Act requires all companies deploying AI models with more than 1 billion parameters to complete mandatory impact assessments covering bias, safety, and societal effects. The law, effective immediately, applies to both local and foreign companies serving Singaporean users, with fines up to $10 million for non-compliance.

The requirements: Companies must submit detailed reports on training data sources, bias testing results, and risk mitigation strategies to Singapore's new AI Safety Authority before deployment. The assessments must be updated quarterly and made publicly available, creating unprecedented transparency requirements that go beyond existing EU and US regulations.

Global implications: Industry experts predict Singapore's model could become the new global standard, similar to how GDPR influenced worldwide privacy regulations. Major AI companies including OpenAI, Anthropic, and Meta are scrambling to set up Singapore compliance teams, while some smaller companies are considering restricting access to Singaporean users rather than meet the requirements.

Research, Translated

AI Learns to Navigate Like Ants Using Smell Instead of GPS

The discovery: Researchers at DeepMind have developed an AI navigation system that mimics how ants find their way using chemical trails, completely eliminating the need for GPS or visual landmarks. The system uses artificial 'smell sensors' that detect and follow chemical gradients, allowing robots to navigate complex indoor environments where GPS fails.

How it works: Instead of creating maps, the AI drops virtual 'pheromone' markers that strengthen successful paths and fade from unsuccessful routes, just like real ant colonies. Multiple robots can share these chemical trails, creating a collaborative navigation network that gets smarter over time as more robots use the same routes.

Real applications: The breakthrough could revolutionize warehouse automation, search and rescue operations, and exploration of GPS-denied environments like underground tunnels or other planets. Early tests show the system is 3x more efficient than traditional robot navigation in cluttered spaces, and it works even when individual robots fail or get lost.

Nature, MIT Technology Review
