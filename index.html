What's Contentious

Oscars Ban AI Actors and Scripts in Landmark Eligibility Ruling

What happened: The Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences released new Oscar eligibility rules on Friday, May 2, explicitly barring AI-generated performances and screenplays from Academy Award consideration. Only performances credited in legal billing and demonstrably performed by humans with their consent will be eligible, and screenplays must be human-authored. The Academy reserves the right to request detailed information about any film's AI usage.

Why it matters: The decision comes as AI-generated performers like Tilly Norwood generate headlines and studios explore synthetic versions of actors including Val Kilmer for independent films. The ruling establishes a clear boundary in Hollywood's most prestigious awards, effectively declaring that creative authorship and performance remain fundamentally human endeavors. The timing reflects industry anxiety as new video generation models become increasingly sophisticated.

What's next: The Academy's stance may influence other awards bodies and production standards across the entertainment industry. While AI tools can still be used in production pipelines for effects or assistance, the core creative elements eligible for recognition must originate from human creators. The policy sets a precedent as the film industry navigates the boundary between AI as a tool versus AI as a replacement for human artistry.

Pentagon Expands Classified AI Access to Microsoft, Amazon, Nvidia

What happened: The Pentagon announced May 1-2 that Microsoft, Amazon, Nvidia, Reflection AI, and Oracle have struck agreements to deploy advanced AI tools on classified military networks for lawful operational use. The expansion significantly broadens the roster of tech companies with access to sensitive defense systems, according to Defense Department statements and officials briefed on the matter.

Why it matters: The agreements signal the Defense Department's push to rapidly integrate frontier AI capabilities into military operations, despite ongoing controversies over AI ethics in warfare. The move comes after Anthropic's high-profile refusal to remove safety restrictions for Pentagon use, creating an opening for competitors willing to accept broader deployment terms. The deals represent billions in potential revenue for participating companies.

What's next: The Pentagon's AI integration is accelerating across classified networks, with additional tech partnerships likely as the military races to deploy AI for intelligence analysis, logistics, and operational planning. The expansion raises questions about oversight and the balance between AI safety considerations and national security imperatives, particularly as companies compete for lucrative defense contracts in an increasingly competitive market.
