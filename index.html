Policy Alert

Canada Bans AI Systems from Making Hiring Decisions Alone

The legislation: Canada's Parliament passed the Algorithmic Accountability Act, prohibiting AI systems from making final hiring, firing, or promotion decisions without human oversight. The law requires companies to disclose AI use in HR processes and gives job candidates the right to request human review of any AI-influenced decision.

The requirements: Companies must conduct bias audits of their AI hiring tools every six months and publish demographic impact reports. HR departments have 180 days to implement human review processes, with violations carrying fines up to $10 million CAD or 3% of global revenue.

The precedent: Canada becomes the first G7 nation to specifically regulate AI in employment decisions, following similar moves by New York City and the EU. US tech companies are watching closely as California considers identical legislation that could reshape hiring practices across North America.

Research, Translated

AI Learns to Forget: New Method Helps Models Unlearn Bad Data

The breakthrough: Researchers at UC Berkeley developed 'selective unlearning,' a technique that lets AI models forget specific information without retraining from scratch. Their paper shows how to remove copyrighted content, personal data, or biased examples from trained models while preserving overall performance.

How it works: Instead of the costly process of retraining entire models, the method identifies and modifies only the neural network weights connected to unwanted information. Think of it like targeted amnesia - the AI forgets specific facts while retaining all other knowledge and capabilities intact.

Why this matters: This could solve major problems with AI systems that have absorbed problematic training data, from copyrighted books to private information scraped from the internet. The technique could help companies comply with 'right to be forgotten' laws and remove harmful biases without starting over from zero.
