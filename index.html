Pentagon Signs AI Deals with 7 Companies, Excluding Anthropic

What happened: The Pentagon announced deals with SpaceX, OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Nvidia, Amazon Web Services and Reflection to deploy AI on classified networks, but Anthropic was excluded after the Trump administration blacklisted it over the company's insistence that the Pentagon include certain safety guardrails for the government's use of AI in warfare. Anthropic refused to back down on terms that would allow the military to use Claude for 'all lawful purposes,' including autonomous weapons and mass surveillance.

Why it matters: Anthropic is missing out on substantial revenue that its competitors have access to, as last year's One Big Beautiful Bill Act included a large sum of money for the Pentagon to spend on AI and offensive cyber operations. The Pentagon declared Anthropic a 'supply chain risk,' a label only used in the past for companies associated with foreign adversaries. A federal judge in California last month blocked the government's effort after Anthropic sued.

What's next: The White House reopened discussions with Anthropic in recent weeks after the company made significant announcements about several technology breakthroughs. Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei visited the White House last month for a meeting with Chief of Staff Susie Wiles after Anthropic unveiled its Mythos tool that can identify cybersecurity threats. The standoff highlights fundamental questions about AI companies' ability to set ethical boundaries on government use.

Money Moves

OpenAI CFO Says Company Sees 'Vertical Wall of Demand'

What happened: OpenAI Chief Financial Officer Sarah Friar pushed back on concerns about missing internal targets, saying the company is meeting objectives and sees 'a vertical wall of demand' for its products. The comments come as OpenAI continues to prepare for a potential IPO later this year while competitors intensify pressure.

Why it matters: The statement directly addresses investor concerns about whether OpenAI can sustain its massive valuation and growth trajectory. With the company having raised over $122 billion in Q1 2026 according to earlier reporting, Friar's bullish assessment suggests OpenAI believes enterprise adoption is accelerating rather than plateauing despite the increasingly crowded AI market.

What's next: OpenAI is reportedly targeting a Q4 2026 IPO at a near-$1 trillion valuation. The company's ability to demonstrate sustained revenue growth and expanding enterprise demand will be critical to justifying that valuation to public market investors. Competitors like Anthropic and Google are also ramping up enterprise offerings, setting up an intense battle for corporate AI budgets.
