What's Hot

Anthropic Commits $200 Billion to Google in Massive Cloud Deal

What happened: Anthropic has agreed to spend $200 billion with Google Cloud over five years starting in 2027, according to The Information. The deal includes access to multiple gigawatts of Google's tensor processing units and accounts for more than 40% of Google's disclosed revenue backlog. Alphabet shares rose approximately 2% following the announcement on May 6, 2026.

Why it matters: This massive commitment highlights the extreme infrastructure costs required to build and run frontier AI models like Claude. Together with similar deals from OpenAI, Anthropic and OpenAI now account for more than half of the $2 trillion in backlogs across major cloud providers including Amazon, Google, Microsoft, and Oracle. The deal solidifies Google's position as a primary infrastructure provider for the AI industry.

What's next: The capacity won't fully come online until 2027, but the arrangement locks in Anthropic's computing power for years ahead. The company is projected to spend upwards of $20 billion on servers in 2026 alone, triple last year's spending. This compute scramble is reshaping competitive dynamics as the largest AI labs secure capacity years in advance, potentially squeezing out smaller players.

What's Contentious

Microsoft May Abandon Clean Energy Goal as AI Demand Soars

What happened: Microsoft is considering delaying or abandoning its 2030 goal of matching 100% of its hourly electricity use with renewable energy purchases, Bloomberg News reported on May 6, 2026. The expensive and energy-intensive push for AI data centers is reshaping the feasibility of climate commitments that were made before the AI era and rank among the tech industry's most ambitious targets.

Why it matters: The potential retreat underscores a structural tension between AI infrastructure expansion and clean energy procurement at scale. Microsoft, Amazon, and Alphabet are spending hundreds of billions of dollars to build AI infrastructure needed to power services like Copilot and Azure. The discussions highlight how the race to dominate artificial intelligence is accelerating faster than the energy systems designed to support it.

What's next: No final decision has been made, and discussions are ongoing. A Microsoft spokesperson pointed to recently signed agreements to bring 1.2 gigawatts of carbon-free energy projects online in Wisconsin. However, if the hourly matching target is adjusted or delayed, it could weaken Microsoft's public positioning on sustainability and set a precedent for how other tech giants handle the collision between AI ambitions and climate pledges.

Policy Alert

Trump Administration Pivots to AI Safety Testing for Frontier Models

What happened: The Center for AI Standards and Innovation announced on May 5, 2026 that Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and xAI will allow the U.S. government to evaluate their AI models before public release. The Commerce Department will conduct pre-deployment evaluations to assess frontier AI capabilities and advance AI security. Previous partnerships with OpenAI and Anthropic from 2024 have been renegotiated to reflect updated directives.

Why it matters: This marks a sharp pivot from the Trump administration's earlier light-touch approach focused on accelerating AI innovation without guardrails. The shift was driven by concerns about Anthropic's Mythos model and its ability to identify and exploit cybersecurity vulnerabilities. As AI systems grow more powerful and potentially risky, officials want to understand their security implications before they hit the market.

What's next: The White House is reportedly considering an executive order to create a formal AI working group that would establish a government review process for new models. Sources indicate the framework is fairly far along, though specific proposals remain fluid with tensions between economic policy voices worried about complicating deployments and national security officials concerned about AI-enabled cyberattacks.
