Policy Alert

UK Mandates Human Override Buttons on All AI Systems by 2027

The legislation: The UK Parliament passed the AI Accountability Act today, requiring all AI systems deployed in Britain to include clearly marked human override capabilities by January 2027. The law applies to everything from chatbots to autonomous vehicles, mandating that humans can immediately stop or redirect AI actions with a single button press or voice command.

The requirements: Companies must design AI systems with 'meaningful human control' at every decision point, conduct quarterly human override drills, and maintain detailed logs of when overrides are used. The legislation also creates criminal liability for executives whose AI systems cause harm when override mechanisms fail or are deliberately disabled.

The industry reaction: Tech companies warn the requirements could make AI systems clunky and expensive, potentially pushing innovation to other countries. However, consumer groups praise the law as essential protection against AI systems that operate beyond human understanding or control. The EU is reportedly considering similar legislation.

Research, Translated

AI Discovers How to Learn New Skills Without Forgetting Old Ones

The breakthrough: Researchers at DeepMind solved the 'catastrophic forgetting' problem that has plagued AI systems for decades. Their new technique, called Elastic Memory Networks, allows AI models to learn new tasks without losing performance on previous ones - essentially giving AI systems the ability to accumulate knowledge like humans do throughout their lives.

How it works: Instead of storing information in fixed neural pathways, the system creates flexible memory networks that can expand and contract based on the importance of different skills. When learning something new, the AI identifies which old memories are still useful and preserves them while allocating new brain space for fresh capabilities.

Why this matters: This could eliminate the need to retrain AI systems from scratch every time they need new capabilities, making AI development faster and cheaper. More importantly, it brings us closer to AI that can truly learn and grow over time, rather than being locked into specific tasks. The research team believes this is a crucial step toward artificial general intelligence.

Nature, MIT Technology Review
