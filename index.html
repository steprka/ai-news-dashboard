Microsoft Copilot Pro Gets Major Upgrade With Real-Time Web Search

What's Contentious

Artists Sue Midjourney Over Training Data Transparency

The lawsuit: A coalition of visual artists filed a class-action lawsuit against Midjourney, alleging the company used millions of copyrighted artworks to train its AI image generator without permission or compensation, demanding full disclosure of training datasets.

The evidence: Plaintiffs claim they can reproduce specific artistic styles and even watermarks through Midjourney prompts, suggesting their work was directly incorporated into the training data. The suit seeks damages and an injunction requiring consent before using copyrighted works.

Industry impact: This case could set precedent for how AI companies handle training data and whether fair use protections apply to machine learning. Similar lawsuits against Stability AI and DeviantArt are being closely watched by both artists and AI developers.

UX Challenges

ChatGPT Users Report Frustration With New 'Thinking' Interface

The problem: OpenAI's latest ChatGPT update includes a visible 'thinking' process where users can see the AI's reasoning steps, but many report it creates anxiety and slows down perceived response times, leading to decreased satisfaction scores in user testing.

User feedback: Early adopters say watching the AI 'think' makes them second-guess responses and creates an uncanny valley effect. Some users report the feature feels like 'looking behind the curtain' in a way that breaks immersion and trust in the AI's capabilities.

Design tension: The update reflects a broader challenge in AI UX design - balancing transparency with user comfort. While showing reasoning can build trust, it may also expose the messy, iterative nature of AI thinking that users prefer to keep hidden.

Policy Alert

EU AI Act Enforcement Begins With First Company Audits

The enforcement: European regulators began conducting their first compliance audits under the EU AI Act, targeting high-risk AI systems in healthcare, finance, and criminal justice. Companies have 60 days to demonstrate compliance or face fines up to 6% of global revenue.

The targets: Initial audits focus on AI systems used for credit scoring, medical diagnosis, and predictive policing across major EU markets. Regulators are particularly scrutinizing algorithmic transparency, bias testing, and human oversight requirements mandated by the new law.

Global implications: These enforcement actions could become the global standard for AI regulation, similar to how GDPR influenced privacy laws worldwide. US companies operating in Europe are scrambling to ensure compliance, with many hiring specialized AI governance teams.

New Tools

Adobe Launches Firefly Video for Enterprise Creative Workflows

The launch: Adobe unveiled Firefly Video, an AI-powered video generation and editing tool integrated directly into Premiere Pro and After Effects. The tool can generate short video clips from text prompts, extend existing footage, and automatically edit sequences based on natural language commands.

Enterprise focus: Unlike consumer AI video tools, Firefly Video is trained exclusively on Adobe Stock footage and public domain content, giving enterprises legal protection against copyright claims. The tool includes detailed provenance tracking and watermarking for generated content.

Creative impact: Early beta users report the tool significantly speeds up pre-visualization and rough cut creation, though human creativity remains essential for final production. Adobe positions this as augmenting rather than replacing creative professionals in video production workflows.

Research, Translated

MIT Develops AI That Learns Physics Like a Toddler

The breakthrough: MIT researchers created an AI system called 'PhysicsNet' that learns basic physics concepts through visual observation, similar to how young children develop intuitive physics. The system can predict object behavior without explicit physics training, outperforming traditional models on spatial reasoning tasks.

How it works: Instead of being programmed with physics equations, PhysicsNet watches thousands of hours of simple interactions - balls rolling, blocks falling, liquids pouring - and develops its own internal models of gravity, momentum, and collision. This 'intuitive physics' approach mirrors human cognitive development.

Why it matters: This research could lead to AI systems that better understand the physical world without massive datasets or explicit programming. Applications include robotics, autonomous vehicles, and virtual reality, where AI needs to predict how objects behave in real-world scenarios.

MIT News, Nature
