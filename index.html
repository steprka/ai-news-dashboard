What's Hot

Anthropic Taps SpaceX's Colossus for 300 Megawatts of AI Compute

What happened: Anthropic announced on May 7 that it will use all compute capacity at SpaceX's Colossus 1 data center in Memphis, gaining access to more than 300 megawatts and over 220,000 Nvidia GPUs within the month. The deal immediately doubled Claude Code's five-hour rate limits for paid subscribers and removed peak-hour restrictions for Pro and Max users.

Why it matters: The partnership addresses Anthropic's explosive growth—CEO Dario Amodei revealed the company grew at 80x year-over-year in Q1 2026, far exceeding their 10x forecast. This capacity crunch had caused widespread user complaints about usage limits. The deal also marks a striking reversal from Elon Musk, who previously called Anthropic "evil" and "misanthropic," but now says he spent time with the team and "no one set off my evil detector."

What's next: Anthropic expressed interest in partnering with SpaceX to develop multiple gigawatts of orbital AI compute capacity—data centers in space. The companies jointly stated that terrestrial power, land, and cooling cannot meet the compute demands of next-generation AI systems on necessary timelines. This joins Anthropic's other massive infrastructure deals, including up to 5 gigawatts with Amazon and Google.

Money Moves

Musk Folds xAI Into SpaceX, Rents Rival Anthropic His Data Center

What happened: Elon Musk announced on May 7 that xAI will be dissolved as a separate company and renamed SpaceXAI, becoming an AI product line within SpaceX. The move comes as SpaceX simultaneously announced it's leasing the entire Colossus 1 data center—originally built for xAI's Grok models—to direct competitor Anthropic. Grok's global daily active users dropped 12.5% from March to April while Claude's surged 44%.

Why it matters: The restructuring follows sustained losses at xAI, which burned through $7.8 billion in the first nine months of 2025—about $28 million per day—while quarterly losses widened from $1 billion in Q1 to $1.46 billion in Q3. Most of xAI's co-founders have departed since the February SpaceX merger. The decision to lease core infrastructure to a rival signals xAI's pivot toward becoming a cloud provider rather than a standalone AI company, potentially dragging on SpaceX's valuation ahead of its planned IPO.

What's next: SpaceX is targeting a June-July 2026 IPO at a reported $1.75 trillion valuation, which would be the largest in history. The combined entity now includes SpaceX's rocket business, Starlink satellite internet, the X social network, and xAI's Grok chatbot. Analysts question whether positioning xAI as infrastructure-for-hire rather than an independent AI contender will support or undermine that ambitious valuation.

New Tools

Microsoft: Global AI Usage Hits 17.8% of Working-Age Population

What happened: Microsoft released its latest Global AI Diffusion Report on May 7, revealing that AI usage increased by 1.5 percentage points in Q1 2026, reaching 17.8% of the world's working-age population. Twenty-six economies now exceed 30% AI adoption rates, with the UAE leading at 70.1%. The U.S. climbed from 24th to 21st place globally with a 31.3% usage rate, while Asian countries saw accelerating adoption driven by improved AI capabilities in Asian languages.

Why it matters: Contrary to widespread predictions of AI-driven job losses, U.S. software developer employment reached 2.2 million in 2025—up 8.5% year-over-year and a record high for the profession. Early Q1 2026 data shows developer employment was 4% higher than the same period in 2025, suggesting AI coding tools are expanding rather than contracting the field. The intensity of use among high-adoption economies also increased, indicating AI is moving beyond experimentation into daily workflows.

What's next: South Korea, Thailand, and Japan showed the greatest adoption movement in Q1, suggesting the AI user base is diversifying beyond English-speaking markets. Microsoft's report tracks population-normalized metrics rather than raw usage numbers, providing a clearer picture of how deeply AI is penetrating different economies. The data challenges the narrative that AI will immediately displace knowledge workers at scale.
