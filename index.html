Policy Alert

Canada Bans AI Systems That Can't Explain Their Decisions

The law: Canada's Parliament passed the Artificial Intelligence Accountability Act, requiring all AI systems used in high-stakes decisions (hiring, lending, healthcare, criminal justice) to provide clear explanations for their outputs. Companies have 18 months to comply or face fines up to 3% of global revenue.

The requirements: The law mandates that AI explanations must be understandable to average citizens, not just technical experts. Companies must also provide an appeals process for AI decisions and maintain detailed logs of how their systems make choices. Third-party auditors will verify compliance annually.

The ripple effect: Major AI companies are scrambling to develop 'explainable AI' versions of their models specifically for the Canadian market. Some firms are considering withdrawing certain AI services from Canada rather than rebuild their systems. Legal experts predict similar laws will spread to other countries, potentially fragmenting the global AI market.

Research, Translated

AI Learns to Think Step-by-Step Like Humans Do

The breakthrough: Researchers at Stanford developed an AI system called 'ChainThought' that mimics human reasoning by breaking complex problems into sequential steps and backtracking when it hits dead ends. Unlike current AI that generates answers in one shot, ChainThought shows its work and can revise its approach mid-problem.

The method: The system uses a novel 'cognitive scaffolding' approach, where AI models are trained to explicitly model their own thinking process. When solving a math problem, for instance, it first identifies what information it needs, then works through sub-problems in logical order, just like a human student would.

The implications: Early tests show ChainThought solves complex reasoning problems 40% more accurately than GPT-4, especially in areas like legal reasoning, scientific analysis, and strategic planning. The research suggests that making AI 'think slower' paradoxically makes it more reliable, potentially solving the hallucination problem that plagues current large language models.
