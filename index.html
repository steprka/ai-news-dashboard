Policy Alert

EU's AI Act Enforcement Begins with First Company Investigations

The crackdown: European regulators launched formal investigations into five AI companies for potential violations of the EU AI Act, which took effect this month. The European AI Office is examining whether ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and two unnamed startups properly assessed risks and implemented safeguards for their 'high-risk' AI systems. Companies face fines up to 7% of global revenue for violations.

The requirements: Under the new law, AI systems used for hiring, credit scoring, law enforcement, and educational evaluation must undergo rigorous testing and human oversight protocols. Companies must also maintain detailed logs of AI decision-making processes and provide clear explanations to users affected by AI systems. The investigations will test whether current industry practices meet these standards.

The precedent: These first enforcement actions will establish how strictly Europe interprets the AI Act and could influence similar regulations worldwide. US companies operating in Europe are scrambling to hire compliance officers and audit their AI systems. Legal experts predict the investigations could take 18 months and result in landmark precedents that shape global AI governance for the next decade.

Research, Translated

AI Learns to Predict Stock Market Crashes by Reading News Emotions

The breakthrough: Researchers at CMU trained an AI system to analyze the emotional tone of financial news and social media posts, successfully predicting market downturns 2-5 days before they occurred. Their model correctly identified 8 out of 10 significant market drops over the past three years by detecting subtle shifts in language that indicate investor anxiety, even when headlines appeared optimistic.

How it works: The AI processes millions of news articles, earnings calls, and social media posts daily, mapping emotional patterns to market movements. It pays special attention to 'hedging language' like 'however' and 'although' that humans use when expressing hidden doubts. The system also tracks unusual patterns in financial jargon usage that precede institutional selling. Unlike traditional technical analysis, it focuses purely on human psychology reflected in language.

Why it matters: This research suggests that collective human emotions leak into our writing in predictable ways that AI can detect before we're consciously aware of our own sentiment shifts. While the researchers emphasize their work is academic, the implications for algorithmic trading are obvious. The findings also raise questions about market stability if emotional prediction becomes widespread—potentially creating feedback loops where AI systems react to their own predictions.

Nature Machine Intelligence, MIT Technology Review
