Policy Alert

EU Demands AI Companies Delete Training Data from European Users

The ruling: The European Data Protection Board issued binding guidance requiring AI companies to delete all training data derived from EU citizens without explicit consent by June 1, 2026. This affects foundational models from OpenAI, Google, Meta, and dozens of smaller companies that scraped European web content, social media posts, and digital communications.

The scope: Legal experts estimate this could force companies to retrain models worth hundreds of billions in compute costs. The ruling covers any personal data that can be traced to EU citizens, including anonymized datasets, translated content, and derivative works created from original European digital content.

The industry response: AI companies are threatening to withdraw services from Europe rather than comply, calling the requirements 'technically impossible to implement retroactively.' Meta has already announced it will shut down AI features for European users, while OpenAI is exploring a separate EU-compliant model trained only on licensed data.

Research, Translated

AI Breakthrough: Neural Networks Learn to Forget Bad Memories

The discovery: Researchers at Stanford developed a technique called 'selective neural forgetting' that allows AI models to deliberately remove specific knowledge without retraining. Unlike current methods that simply block outputs, this approach actually erases the unwanted information from the model's neural pathways, similar to how human brains suppress traumatic memories.

How it works: The technique identifies which neurons store particular pieces of information, then uses targeted electromagnetic pulses (in hardware) or gradient-based interventions (in software) to selectively weaken those neural connections. The model retains all other knowledge while completely losing access to the targeted information.

Why it matters: This could solve major AI problems like removing copyrighted content from training data, eliminating biased associations, or helping AI systems 'unlearn' harmful information without starting over. The technology could also lead to more human-like AI that can move past negative experiences and adapt its knowledge base dynamically.

Nature, MIT Technology Review
