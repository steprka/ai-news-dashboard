Money Moves

OpenAI and Anthropic Launch Rival Enterprise AI Joint Ventures

What happened: On Monday, Anthropic announced a joint venture with Blackstone, Hellman & Friedman, and Goldman Sachs valued at $1.5 billion to deploy enterprise AI services. Hours before, OpenAI raised more than $4 billion from TPG, Brookfield Asset Management, Advent, and Bain Capital for a similar venture called The Deployment Company at a $10 billion valuation. Both ventures aim to help businesses adopt AI through alternative asset managers who will get preferred access to deploy AI at their portfolio companies.

Why it matters: The ventures follow the same logic: raising money from alternative asset managers to create new channels for enterprise AI deals, where investors will capture more value from resulting contracts while allowing more engineering resources for individual deployments, embracing the forward-deployed engineer model popularized by Palantir. This represents a new distribution strategy where private equity becomes the sales channel, bypassing traditional enterprise sales cycles entirely.

What's next: The ventures come as both AI labs fundraise at a blistering pace while circling possible IPOs, with OpenAI announcing $122 billion in new funding at an $852 billion valuation in March and Anthropic seeking $50 billion at a $900 billion valuation. The race is now on to see which model wins: OpenAI's larger $10 billion bet or Anthropic's more focused $1.5 billion approach.

AI Customer Service Startup Sierra Raises $950M at $15B Valuation

What happened: Eight months after closing a $350 million funding round, Sierra Technologies announced it has raised an additional $950 million at a $15 billion valuation, with Alphabet's GV venture capital arm and Tiger Global leading the investment. The Series E funding round was led by Tiger and Google's GV, with participation from Benchmark, Sequoia, and Greenoaks, as one investor called Sierra the winner in the customer experience category by objective measures like scale of revenue and quality of customer base.

Why it matters: CEO Bret Taylor estimated that $400 billion is spent annually on customer service, and a bulk of that is moving to AI agents. Deals of this size have come to define the recent venture landscape as investors flock to category leaders, with appetite to back names beyond OpenAI and Anthropic, whose valuations are creeping towards $1 trillion, with AI coding agent companies like Cursor and Replit representing the largest area of the market, followed by customer service agents.

What's next: The startup has been able to court and quickly onboard traditional companies that aren't always as quick on tech adoption, with one investor noting that industries that historically have been slower to adopt are realizing that a watchful, waiting approach in AI is a path to extinction. Sierra's massive funding should help it maintain its lead in the increasingly competitive AI customer service market.

Policy Alert

White House Weighs AI Model Review Before Release, NYT Reports

What happened: The Trump administration is considering an executive order to create a working group on artificial intelligence, according to the New York Times, with one proposal including a government review process for new models, with White House officials telling executives from Anthropic, Google, and OpenAI about some of the plans under consideration during meetings last week. The initiative could involve agencies such as the NSA, the White House Office of the National Cyber Director, and the Office of the Director of National Intelligence, with concerns about Anthropic's new Mythos model and its cybersecurity capabilities helping prompt the discussions.

Why it matters: This marks a significant shift in the administration's AI policy. The White House previously pushed for federal preemption of state AI laws and a light-touch regulatory approach, but national security concerns over frontier capabilities appear to be driving a more interventionist stance. A formal pre-release review process would dramatically impact AI companies' development timelines and internal compliance processes.

What's next: A White House official declined to confirm or deny the report and said any policy announcement would come from the president. The industry will be watching whether the White House issues formal executive order text, which agencies are explicitly named, and whether any technical standards or testing protocols become public—all of which would signal the scope and legal mechanisms of federal AI oversight.

What's Contentious

'This Is Fine' Creator Says AI Startup Stole His Iconic Meme

What happened: KC Green, the artist who created the iconic "This is fine" comic featuring an anthropomorphic dog surrounded by flames, said his art was stolen by AI startup Artisan for an ad campaign, with a subway station ad appearing to show Green's art modified to say the dog's pipeline is on fire while urging passersby to hire Ava the AI BDR. Green told followers it's not anything he agreed to, that it's been stolen like AI steals, and urged people to vandalize it if they see it.

Why it matters: When TechCrunch asked Artisan about the ad, the company said they have respect for KC Green and his work and are reaching out to him directly. Artisan has courted controversy with its ads before, specifically with billboards urging businesses to Stop hiring humans, although founder Jaspar Carmichael-Jack insisted that the message was about a category of work, not humans at large. This incident highlights ongoing tensions between AI companies and artists over intellectual property rights.

What's next: The company said it had scheduled time to speak with Green directly. This case adds to the growing list of IP disputes between AI companies and content creators, coming as Congress and courts grapple with how copyright law applies to AI training data and AI-generated content. The incident may fuel further scrutiny of how AI startups source and use creative work in their marketing.

The Discourse

Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang Says AI Creating Jobs Amid Labor Worries

What happened: During a conversation Monday night with MSNBC's Becky Quick hosted by the Milken Institute, Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang said that AI was an industrial-scale generator of jobs, not the harbinger of mass unemployment that AI doomers have often accused it of being. A number of different topics were broached during the talk, but a central theme that kept coming back was the ongoing economic anxiety surrounding the AI industry and whether it was something Americans should be legitimately worried about.

Why it matters: Huang's optimistic framing contrasts sharply with widespread worker anxiety about AI displacement. The debate comes as companies increasingly deploy AI agents for customer service, coding, and other knowledge work tasks. While Huang argues AI creates opportunities, critics point to layoffs at tech companies and automation of entry-level positions as evidence that AI's labor impact is more complex than industry leaders admit.

What's next: The tension between AI boosterism and worker concerns will likely intensify as more AI agents move from pilots to production. With both OpenAI and Anthropic now launching enterprise deployment ventures specifically designed to accelerate AI adoption in traditional industries, the real-world employment effects will become harder to ignore—and the discourse around AI and jobs will shift from prediction to documentation.

Research, Translated

Harvard Study: AI More Accurate Than ER Doctors at Diagnoses

What happened: A new study from Harvard examines how large language models perform in a variety of medical contexts, including real emergency room cases, where at least one model seemed to be more accurate than human doctors. The research tested AI systems on actual emergency room cases and compared their diagnostic accuracy to practicing physicians, with AI models outperforming human clinicians in multiple scenarios.

Why it matters: Emergency room diagnosis represents one of the most challenging and high-stakes medical contexts, where doctors must make rapid decisions with incomplete information under time pressure. If AI can genuinely outperform human physicians in this setting, it suggests the technology may be ready for clinical deployment in ways that go beyond administrative tasks or simple triage. However, the study also raises questions about how to integrate AI into medical workflows while maintaining human oversight and accountability.

What's next: The findings will likely accelerate adoption of AI diagnostic tools in hospital settings, but implementation will require careful navigation of liability, regulatory approval, and physician trust. Medical AI companies have raised billions in funding, and studies like this provide the clinical validation they need to move from pilots to deployment. Expect increased scrutiny of how these tools perform across different patient populations and medical conditions.
