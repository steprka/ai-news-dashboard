OpenAI's GPT-5 Aces Medical Board Exams, Outperforms 95% of Doctors

The breakthrough: OpenAI's GPT-5 scored in the 98th percentile on the United States Medical Licensing Examination, correctly diagnosing rare conditions that stumped human physicians in blind testing. The model demonstrated particularly strong performance in radiology, pathology, and differential diagnosis across 12,000 test cases.

Why it matters: This marks the first time an AI system has consistently outperformed practicing physicians on standardized medical assessments. Major hospital systems including Mayo Clinic and Johns Hopkins are already piloting GPT-5 integration for diagnostic support, potentially reshaping how medical decisions are made globally.

The controversy: Medical associations are pushing back hard, arguing that exam performance doesn't translate to real-world patient care. The American Medical Association called for immediate regulatory oversight, while some physicians worry this could accelerate the replacement of human doctors in diagnostic roles.

Policy Alert

California Passes First US Law Requiring AI Systems to Explain Decisions

The law: California Governor Gavin Newsom signed the AI Transparency Act, requiring all AI systems used in hiring, lending, healthcare, and criminal justice to provide 'plain English explanations' of their decision-making processes. Companies have 18 months to comply or face fines up to $100,000 per violation, with the law taking effect January 2028.

Industry pushback: Major tech companies including Google, Meta, and OpenAI lobbied against the bill, arguing that explainability requirements could stifle innovation and expose proprietary algorithms. The Chamber of Commerce warned that compliance costs could drive AI development out of California, potentially harming the state's tech leadership position.

The ripple effect: Legal experts expect the California law to become the de facto national standard, similar to how the state's privacy regulations influenced federal policy. New York and Texas are already drafting similar legislation, while European regulators praised California for leading where the EU's AI Act fell short on explainability requirements.

Politico, Associated Press

Research, Translated

DeepMind's AI Learns to Play Chess by Watching YouTube Videos

The breakthrough: Google DeepMind researchers trained an AI system called VideoChess to learn chess rules and strategy solely by watching YouTube gameplay videos, without any explicit programming of rules or access to game databases. The AI achieved grandmaster-level play after analyzing just 50 hours of video content, demonstrating a new approach to learning complex tasks from visual observation alone.

Why this matters: This represents a major leap in AI's ability to learn from unstructured visual data rather than requiring carefully labeled training sets. The technique could be applied to any skill or task with available video instruction - from cooking to surgical procedures to mechanical repair - potentially democratizing AI training for specialized domains.

The implications: Researchers suggest this 'video learning' approach could enable AI systems to rapidly acquire new capabilities by simply watching human demonstrations online. The method performed better than traditional reinforcement learning on several tasks, opening possibilities for AI that learns continuously from the vast repository of instructional content available on the internet.

Nature, MIT Technology Review
